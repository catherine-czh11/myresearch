After a strong rally to near its 52-week high, the shares are now fairly valued rather than the bargain they were at the lows: our DCF estimate of intrinsic value, about $156 a share, sits level with the price. OC's headline discount to building-products peers (8.6x vs ~14x EV/EBITDA) is real, but it is structural — a long-standing feature, not a catalyst. The case for owning it now rests on cash quality and optionality: a sector-high ~6.6% free-cash-flow yield, self-funded deleveraging toward ~1.0x, and a possible recovery in Doors margins and housing — most of which is already reflected in today's 8.6x. With no valuation cushion and the stock technically extended, the stance is to wait for a pullback.

What it is

Owens Corning sells through three reportable segments. Roofing is the biggest profit engine, Doors the smallest and lowest-margin, and how the mix shifts between them is the central margin story.

Roofing — 43% rev · 57% profit

Asphalt shingles and roofing components; the #2 U.S. shingle producer, vertically integrated in asphalt and glass mat. Demand is repair-and-remodel- and storm-driven rather than tied to new construction — the highest-margin, most cash-generative leg, at a ~32% EBITDA margin.

Insulation — 36% rev · 34% profit

Fiberglass, foam and stone-wool insulation; North America's largest fiberglass-insulation producer. Levered to housing starts, remodeling and tightening energy codes, with solid margins in the low-20s (~23% EBITDA margin).

Doors — 21% rev · 9% profit

Interior and exterior residential doors (Masonite), among North America's largest. Most exposed to new-residential construction and the weakest-margin segment (~11%); it absorbed the $1.2bn impairment.

Valuation

Our DCF, anchored to a 7.5x exit multiple — the upper half of OC's own historical 4.5–8.0x range — puts intrinsic value at about $156, level with the price, so there is no margin of safety. A through-cycle-average multiple (~6–6.5x) would imply roughly $123–140, while it takes 9–10x — above anything OC has closed a year at — to reach the $190+ "cheap" zone. On relative multiples OC trades at 8.6x EV/EBITDA versus a ~14.6x core-peer median, but that gap is structural rather than a catalyst. The genuine attraction is a sector-high free-cash-flow yield of ~6.6%, about 225bps above the 10-year Treasury.

What's already priced in

  • The peer discount is understood, not a hidden gap: at 8.6x vs ~14x, the market already reflects OC's higher cyclicality, ~2.5x leverage and the margin-dilutive Doors mix — so the discount is unlikely to close on its own.
  • The cash story is in the price: the sector-high ~6.6% free-cash-flow yield and self-funded deleveraging toward ~1.0x are already captured in today's 8.6x multiple.
  • The upside options aren't free: a Doors-margin recovery (11% → mid-teens) and a stronger housing/R&R upcycle drive the bull case (~$216), but neither is required for fair value and neither is yet proven.

Main risks

  • Valuation at the top of its range: at 8.6x OC sits above its FY2021–25 year-end multiples; reversion toward the ~6–7x through-cycle average implies $123–145 — downside to the current price.
  • No margin of safety: intrinsic value roughly equals the price on the base case, and the DCF is ~74% terminal value.
  • Housing and repair-remodel cyclicality and rate sensitivity drive a ~$86 bear case; and the unproven Doors/Masonite margin recovery — a ~$3.9bn deal that already took a $1.2bn impairment — is central to the return on that capital.

Bottom line

OC is fair value: the 7.5x-exit DCF (~$156) sits level with the price, and the wide peer discount is structural rather than a catalyst to close. With no valuation cushion and the shares technically extended — overbought, RSI 72, about 29% above a rising 100-day average near the 52-week high — the stance is to wait. A pullback toward the 100-day average (~$121–140) would restore both a margin of safety below the $156 base and a cleaner technical entry. The bull case (~$216) needs an operational upturn or re-rating; the bear (~$86) is a cycle trough.
