CMC is the biggest and lowest-cost maker of steel reinforcing bar (rebar) in the United States, and it is reshaping itself by buying into higher-margin, less cyclical precast-concrete products. At about $61.73 the market is paying for today's strong steel margins to last, while giving little credit for the precast shift. We think the shares are fairly valued: attractive versus rival steelmakers, but not cheap against the cash the business generates once steel margins normalize.

What it is

CMC turns recycled scrap metal into steel and construction products through three business units:

North America Steel

The core: recycling yards feed electric-arc-furnace mills that make rebar and merchant bar, plus fabrication plants that shape it for building sites. About three-quarters of sales, ~14% profit margin.

Construction Solutions

The growth engine: precast-concrete products (recently acquired), Tensar ground-stabilization and value-added reinforcing. Higher and steadier margins (~20%+) than steel.

Europe Steel

A Polish mill and fabrication network, recovering as new EU trade rules support local demand; the smallest and most cyclical unit (~12% margin now).

Valuation

Our cash-flow model puts base-case value at about $52 per share, within a wide range of roughly $24 (if steel margins fall hard) to $74 (if they stay strong) — the current $62 price sits in the upper half. Against rival steelmakers CMC looks cheap: 7.7x earnings-before-interest-tax-depreciation (EV/EBITDA) versus about 11x for Nucor and Steel Dynamics, and a 5.9% free-cash-flow yield. The gap reflects CMC's smaller size, its fresh debt from the precast deals, and an unresolved lawsuit.

What's already priced in

  • Today's steel margin (about $610 per ton) is near a three-year high; the price assumes it stays well above the $430–490 per ton we treat as normal through the cycle.
  • The market gives little credit yet for the precast acquisitions, which add steadier profits — this is the main way the shares could re-rate higher toward peer valuations.
  • A wave of new U.S. rebar capacity (~2–3 million tons) is arriving over the next few years; if demand keeps growing ~3% a year it is absorbable, but it caps how high margins can run.

Main risks

  • Steel margins fall toward $350–400 per ton as new capacity ramps into softer demand — this alone could cut our value estimate to about $24.
  • The Pacific Steel Group lawsuit ($373m accrued) is resolved worse than the ~$330m cash payment we assume.
  • Integrating $2.5bn of precast acquisitions and finishing a new West Virginia mill strain a balance sheet that now carries more debt.

Bottom line

Fair value, and we would wait. The shares sit between our base ($52) and optimistic ($74) estimates while the recent price trend is still weak. We would look to buy nearer $52–55, or on clear signs the trend has turned; the company's 5 August 2026 Investor Day is the next event likely to reset expectations.
