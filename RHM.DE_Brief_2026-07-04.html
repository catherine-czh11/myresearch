Fairly priced today; wait for a better level or a repaired trend before buying.

Rheinmetall is the main listed way to invest in Europe's rearmament. Its record €63.8bn order backlog — about 6.4× a year's revenue — means the next few years of growth are largely already contracted. The shares have fallen ~37% over the past year after Germany cancelled the F126 frigate programme and defence stocks pulled back, leaving them roughly fairly valued: in line with peers on next-year earnings, and near the cautious end of what the business is worth. The big upside case only works if the company delivers its aggressive 2030 ambition — so the reward is optionality on execution, not a cheap price today.

What it is

Rheinmetall is Germany's largest defence company. It has become an almost pure defence business (it is selling its old civilian car-parts division) and makes money across three areas:

Vehicle Systems

Armoured and tactical military vehicles — the biggest sales line but the lowest margin (~15% EBITDA margin), improving as volumes grow.

Weapon & Ammunition

Artillery, ammunition and propellants — the profit engine: ~a third of sales but half of profit, at a ~34% EBITDA margin.

Electronic Solutions

Air defence, sensors and mission systems — the fastest-growing area (sales up ~44%), at a ~17% EBITDA margin.

Valuation

On next-year earnings the shares trade at ~20× — roughly the same as defence peers — and the price sits close to the cautious ("bear") estimate of intrinsic value (~€1,061), which is about where the stock is now. The more aggressive base case, which assumes the company grows toward its ~€50bn 2030 ambition, points to ~€1,835 (about 70% above the price) — but that value rests almost entirely on assumptions about the distant future. On one common measure (EV/EBITDA of ~24.9×) the stock is at a ~50% premium to peers, justified by faster growth and higher margins but leaving little room for disappointment. Its cash return (FCF yield ~1.7%) is low for now because the company is spending heavily to expand.

What's already priced in

  • The market is paying for a cautious, steady future — not the company's aggressive €50bn-by-2030 ambition; that upside is upside, not baked into today's price.
  • The EV/EBITDA premium already reflects Rheinmetall's superior growth and the highest profit margin in its peer group — so the "quality" is recognised.
  • Most of the intrinsic-value estimate depends on the terminal (post-2030) assumptions, so the valuation swings a lot on beliefs about how long the rearmament boom lasts.

Main risks

  • Profit margins in the high-margin ammunition business could fall as European rivals add capacity — the single biggest downside driver.
  • Single-programme/political risk: the F126 cancellation shows even a rising-budget environment can bring setbacks; export approvals can also constrain deliveries.
  • The shares are richly priced on EV/EBITDA (~50% above peers), so any growth or margin disappointment could de-rate them.

Bottom line

Rheinmetall is a high-quality, well-financed (net cash) leader in a multi-year rearmament trend, but the shares are fairly — not cheaply — valued and are technically in a downtrend after the F126 news, ~17.5% below their 100-day average. There is no rush: prefer buying nearer support (~€929–€1,000) on further weakness, or once the trend clearly repairs. The upside case is a bet on the company delivering its 2030 growth ambition.
