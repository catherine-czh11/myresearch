Amkor is a high-quality but thin-margin, capital-heavy back-end chip manufacturer whose shares have run far ahead of the cash the business actually generates. The market is treating an AI/advanced-packaging boom as permanent: at $65.33 the stock effectively pays about twice Amkor's own long-run valuation. Every scenario in our intrinsic model — from a bearish $16.81 to a bullish $40.72 — comes out below today's price, with a base case of $28.04 implying roughly 57% downside. The business itself is sound and shifting toward the right, higher-value work; the problem is simply that the price already assumes a steady state the fundamentals do not yet deliver.

What it is

Amkor is the world's largest US-headquartered outsourced semiconductor assembly and test provider (OSAT) — in plain terms, a for-hire factory that packages and tests the chips other companies design. Customers ship in their finished silicon wafers; Amkor encloses the tiny die in a usable package, tests it, and ships the finished units back for a per-unit fee. It runs as a single business, but reports its sales in two product groups:

Advanced Products (~83% of sales)

The high-value, AI-exposed lines — flip-chip, 2.5D packaging for GPUs and high-bandwidth memory, and wafer-level packages. This is the growth engine (revenue up ~6%/yr over 2021–25) and the reason to own the stock.

Mainstream Products (~17% of sales)

The mature lines — traditional wirebond, power and sensor packages. A steady but shrinking business (revenue down ~10%/yr over 2021–25) as the mix keeps tilting toward Advanced.

Valuation

Our discounted-cash-flow model puts fair value at about $28 per share in the base case, within a range of roughly $17 (bear) to $41 (bull) — all below the $65.33 market price. To justify today's price, the shares would need Amkor to hold a through-cycle valuation of about 12.4x EV/EBITDA forever, versus its own ~6.2x five-year average. On headline multiples Amkor looks cheap — 13.2x EV/EBITDA against a 22.2x peer median — but that "discount" only exists because the Asian packaging peers have re-rated even harder on AI; measured against Amkor's own history, 13.2x is roughly a 2x premium. The free-cash-flow yield is just ~1%, partly because the company is spending heavily on new capacity right now.

Why it's expensive — and what would have to go right

  • The price assumes the AI boom is permanent. Today's shares bake in roughly double Amkor's normal long-run valuation; if the multiple drifts back toward history — as it has before — the stock de-rates sharply even if the business does fine.
  • Cash comes later, not now. Base-case free cash flow is negative in 2026 as Amkor pours money into its ~$7bn Arizona campus and other capacity; about 87% of the model's value sits beyond 2030, so the payoff depends on a harvest phase that is still years away.
  • Management's own targets would need to be met and then some. Amkor's Investor Day lays out "3x earnings power by 2030" (~$11bn revenue, ~22%+ gross margin, ~$5+ EPS). Even hitting those aspirational goals gets the bull case only to ~$41 — still below today's price — so the current valuation needs execution beyond the plan.

Main risks

  • Valuation is the biggest risk. The price discounts a permanent re-rating the base case doesn't support; a cyclical air-pocket unwinds it fast — the stock already round-tripped from ~$97 to ~$55 within a single month.
  • Customer concentration. Apple is ~30% of sales and the top-10 customers ~72%, with no long-term volume commitments; losing a socket or an iPhone-cycle downturn would be a material earnings event.
  • Spending and timing. $2.5–3.0bn of 2026 capex drives negative free cash flow; a slower Arizona fill or margin ramp pushes the payoff further out and pressures the long-dated value that carries the stock.

Bottom line

Good business, wrong price. Amkor trades about 57% above our base-case fair value, and even the optimistic case sits well under today's $65.33, so there is no case to buy on valuation alone. The chart offers no offsetting timing signal — the shares are stalled around their 100-day average with mixed momentum. We would revisit only if the price falls back toward the $28–$41 intrinsic range.
