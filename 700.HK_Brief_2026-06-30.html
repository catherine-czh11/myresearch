What it is

Tencent runs China's dominant online platform, built around the WeChat/Weixin app used by about 1.4 billion people every month. It makes money in three main ways:

Games & social

The biggest and most profitable slice — about half of sales. Blockbuster games such as Honour of Kings, plus paid memberships, music and video.

Payments & cloud

About a third of sales. WeChat Pay handles a huge share of China's mobile payments, alongside a growing cloud-computing business.

Advertising

About a fifth of sales, and the fastest-growing, highest-margin part — ads across WeChat, short video and mini-programs.

Valuation

On our discounted cash-flow model, Tencent is worth about HKD 586 a share in the base case, within a range of roughly HKD 513–652 — around 39% above the recent HKD 421 price. It trades at about 9 times EBITDA and 11 times earnings, broadly in line with less-profitable rivals despite far higher margins and stronger cash generation. Crucially, that value counts only the core business and net cash — it gives no credit for Tencent's roughly RMB 985 billion portfolio of stakes in other companies (about 30% of its market value).

Why it's cheap — and why that should change

  • The market is applying a heavy China/geopolitical risk discount — effectively demanding an ~11% return hurdle, well above what the cash flows justify. As those fears ease, the shares can re-rate.
  • The price gives almost no credit for Tencent's large portfolio of investments in other listed and private companies (~RMB 985 billion) — a store of value sitting outside the share price.
  • Profits are growing faster than sales as the mix shifts toward high-margin games, ads and video; a current spike in AI-related spending that is depressing free cash flow should ease over the next few years, while ongoing buybacks lift value per share.

Main risks

  • China policy and US-China tensions — game-approval rules, a US government list that named Tencent, and limits on buying advanced AI chips — can cap the shares regardless of how the business performs.
  • Heavy AI investment could stay high for longer with an uncertain payoff, holding back free cash flow.
  • The value of Tencent's investment portfolio swings sharply, and a large shareholder (Prosus/Naspers) keeps selling stock, weighing on the price.

Bottom line

Tencent looks meaningfully undervalued for a patient, long-term investor: the business is high-quality and the price is low relative to what we think it is worth. But the shares are in a downtrend, so rather than chase, a sensible approach is to build a position gradually near current levels (around the HKD 411 support) and add once the price steadies and turns up. We rate it Attractive — and set no price target.
