Cognex is the world's co-leader in industrial machine vision — the high-margin, debt-free business that makes the systems letting factory and warehouse equipment "see." It is midway through a strong cyclical recovery and a management-led margin improvement, and its quality is not in doubt. The debate is entirely about price. At $69.49 the shares trade at roughly three times our base-case estimate of intrinsic worth ($23.96) and at a premium to close peers, with a free-cash-flow yield (~2.1%) that sits below the risk-free rate. The market is valuing Cognex like a premium growth franchise — similar to Japan's Keyence — rather than on the more conservative cash-flow model behind our fair value. Our verdict is Expensive: the operating momentum is genuine and our DCF terminal is deliberately cautious, but on every lens — intrinsic value, peer multiples and cash yield — risk and reward skew unfavourably here.

What it is

Cognex makes the "eyes" of factories and warehouses — hardware-plus-software systems that let machines locate, identify, inspect, measure and read items on production and distribution lines. It reports as a single business (machine vision), sells mostly one-time hardware and software rather than subscriptions, and earns a very high gross margin (~69%). Because it runs as one segment, the split below is by product family rather than a formal profit breakdown.

Vision systems & software

The core franchise — In-Sight vision systems and sensors plus VisionPro and the newer OneVision AI software. This is the software-rich, highest-value layer where Cognex captures the most margin and competes head-to-head with Keyence.

Barcode / ID readers (DataMan)

Image-based barcode readers central to the logistics and e-commerce business — the largest and fastest-growing end market, where machine-vision penetration is still only around 15%.

Valuation

Our base-case discounted-cash-flow value is $23.96 a share (range $20.25–$34.47), against the $69.49 market price — so the shares carry no cushion on intrinsic worth. On peer multiples the premium is real but milder: 43.8x EV/EBITDA versus a 25.3x core-peer median (flattered by ~$622m of net cash and an adjusted-EBITDA basis), while the cleaner forward P/E of 39.3x is only about 15% above peers. The ~2.1% free-cash-flow yield sits below both the 10-year Treasury and the ~10% cost of equity — the stock is priced for growth, not today's cash generation. One important caveat: roughly three-quarters of our DCF value rests in its terminal assumption, which implies only ~10.7x exit EV/EBITDA — deliberately conservative for a franchise of this quality — so the literal 3x gap overstates the case.

Why it's expensive — and what would have to go right

  • The entire premium is a bet on a peer-level terminal multiple. No reasonable discount-rate/growth combination in the cash-flow model reaches $69.49 — the market is paying for a Keyence-like ~44x EBITDA multiple to persist.
  • For that to be justified, the raised 25–31% through-cycle margin band and 13–14% growth ambition set at the June-2025 Investor Day must prove durable through the next downturn, not just this recovery. Seven straight quarters of margin expansion make it credible but unproven.
  • Even so, the honest read is "richly priced," not "3x overvalued": ~75% of the DCF sits in a conservative ~10.7x terminal, well below the ~14–15x a quality franchise has historically cleared — normalising that narrows, though does not close, the gap.

Main risks

  • Valuation air-gap: at 43.8x EV/EBITDA and a below-risk-free cash yield, any earnings cut hits both the profits and the multiple at the same time.
  • Margin durability is unproven through a downturn — today's mid-20s% margin blends genuine cost-out with cyclical recovery, and the raised band is tested by the next downcycle.
  • AI and low-cost Chinese entrants could erode software pricing and differentiation over time; demand is also cyclical and capex-linked with little backlog, and China adds export-control, tariff and Taiwan chip-supply risk.

Bottom line

A high-quality business at a demanding price. The charts look constructive — a confirmed uptrend — but with the stock near its 52-week high, trading at a premium on every valuation lens and roughly three times our base intrinsic value, there is essentially no margin of safety. We would defer new buying and treat price strength as a chance to trim rather than add. A pullback toward the ~$58–65 support zone would improve the setup, though even that remains well above our fair-value estimate.
