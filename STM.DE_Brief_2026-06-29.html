Even assuming the company hits its own ambitious medium-term targets, our estimate of what the business is worth sits well below today's price, and the shares generate very little cash relative to what you pay for them. What it is

STMicroelectronics designs and manufactures semiconductors — the chips inside cars, factories, phones and electronic devices. Unlike companies that only design chips, ST also runs its own factories, which gives it control and differentiation but makes it capital-heavy, so profits swing sharply with how full those factories are. Its business splits into two main product groups:

Analog, sensors & power (APMS)

Analog chips, MEMS motion and environmental sensors, and power/discrete components for cars and industry. ST's largest group and a core profit engine — though its power-and-discrete part is currently loss-making in the downturn.

Microcontrollers & connectivity (MDRF)

The STM32 microcontroller family (tiny programmable computers-on-a-chip) plus radio-frequency and optical-communication chips, including parts for AI data centres. The highest-margin group.

Valuation

We value the shares at roughly $47 each (a range of about $36–57), using a discounted-cash-flow model that already assumes the company achieves its own ambitious recovery targets — and that is well below the ~$74 price. Other measures point the same way: investors pay a higher multiple of next year's earnings than for most peers, and the shares throw off only about 0.5% in free cash each year, far less than the ~11% return we would require to own a business this cyclical (or even a safe government bond at ~4%).

Why it's expensive — and what would have to go right

  • The price already banks a full recovery. Even assuming ST reaches its own targets — revenue back near $18bn and a 22–24% operating margin by 2027–28 — our fair value still sits below today's price.
  • To be worth ~$74, the market is effectively betting ST holds near peak-cycle profitability indefinitely and earns the higher valuation multiples of premium chip designers — a demanding combination.
  • What would have to go right: the AI/data-centre business ramps faster than planned, the car and industrial recovery is sharper than expected, and the company puts its large and growing cash pile to work in value-creating buybacks or acquisitions.

Main risks

  • Valuation — the stock is priced for the full recovery plus a re-rating; if either disappoints, it has room to fall.
  • Execution — the 22–24% margin goal is ambitious against a high European cost base and a currently loss-making power-chip unit.
  • Cyclical and political — a renewed slowdown (tariffs, China demand, slower electric-vehicle uptake) would stall the recovery, and French/Italian government control (~28% combined) adds governance friction.

Bottom line

A good company at a rich price. The business is high quality and financially rock-solid — it holds more cash than debt — but the shares have already round-tripped back near their highs and trade above what we think the business is worth, even on optimistic assumptions. We would wait for a meaningful pullback before considering an entry rather than buying at today's levels.
