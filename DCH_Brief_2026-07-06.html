Attractive on valuation, but wait on timing — the shares look undervalued, yet the chart is still in a downtrend, so there is no rush to buy.

Dauch Corporation is the former American Axle, transformed in February 2026 by its acquisition of Dowlais (the GKN Automotive and GKN Powder Metallurgy businesses) into a roughly $10.4 billion-revenue supplier of driveline and metal-formed parts for carmakers. The market is pricing the stock as a distressed, over-indebted auto supplier that won't deliver its merger savings — and we think that is too harsh. The investment case rests on two levers management already controls: roughly $300 million of combination cost savings and steadily paying down a ~$4 billion net-debt pile. Debt paydown mechanically shifts value from lenders to shareholders even if the business merely treads water, and our numbers show meaningful upside even though our earnings forecasts sit well below Wall Street's.

What it is

Dauch reports two segments — Driveline and Metal Forming — with GKN Automotive folded into Driveline and GKN Powder Metallurgy into Metal Forming. Driveline is both the larger and the more profitable side of the house: it produces 64% of segment revenue but 76% of segment Adjusted EBITDA.

Driveline

Axles, sideshafts and CV joints (now including GKN Automotive). The larger, higher-margin segment — a 13.9% Adjusted EBITDA margin, above the group average — carrying a bigger share of profit than of revenue.

Metal Forming

Forged and powder-metal components (now including GKN Powder Metallurgy). The lower-margin, more cyclical unit — margins fell from ~14.5% in FY2021 to ~6–8% in FY2023–25, and its recovery is a key thing to watch.

Valuation

Our discounted-cash-flow work values the business at $18.13 per share on the whole-company (unlevered) view — about +229% versus the $5.51 share price — and $9.79 per share (+78%) on the more conservative equity-only cross-check that fully reflects the debt load. The stock also screens cheap on relative measures: 6.1x EV/EBITDA versus a 7.1x median for its closest peers, a 7.2x forward P/E versus 9.5x, and a 13.0% free-cash-flow yield that clears the company's ~11.4% cost of capital. Notably, that upside is generated on our forecasts, which sit materially below Wall Street consensus ($0.36 vs $0.76 FY2026 EPS) — the case does not depend on the Street being right.

Why it's cheap — and why that should change

  • The market is pricing distress. Today's price implies a ~16.2% discount rate or roughly −6% perpetual decline — a scenario in which the merger savings never arrive. That is far harsher than the reconciled base case.
  • Debt paydown does the heavy lifting. Net leverage falls from ~3.0x (FY2026E) to ~1.6x (FY2030E) on scheduled repayment; on ~$4 billion of net debt, each turn of deleveraging accrues straight to shareholders even with flat profits.
  • Merger savings are a free option. The base case assumes most of the ~$300m synergies get competed away (a 14.0% terminal margin); actually keeping them (the 16.9% bull path) adds roughly $10 per share on top.

Main risks

  • Heavy debt makes the stock a levered bet. With ~$4 billion of net debt, the equity-only bear case ($4.57 per share) is the one reading below today's price — things going wrong hurts disproportionately.
  • Cyclical and EV-transition exposure. Two-thirds of the valuation sits in the long-term "terminal" years; erosion of combustion-engine content (especially in Metal Forming) or a North American truck downturn would validate the market's pessimism.
  • Integration execution. The ~$300m savings target runs over three years with $110–150m of restructuring cash costs in FY2026; slippage combined with a stuck Metal Forming segment is the bear case.

Bottom line

Attractive on valuation, but don't chase. Intrinsic value exceeds the share price across the base and bull cases, the stock is cheaper than its direct peers, and free cash flow more than covers the cost of capital — yet the chart is in a confirmed downtrend. The sensible approach is to accumulate on weakness toward the $4.92–$5.13 support zone, or to buy on a convincing, volume-backed break above the $6.14 100-day moving average.
